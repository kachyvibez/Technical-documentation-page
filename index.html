How to Start Music Production

Getting Production Equipments
  1. Get a laptop.
  2. Get a midi keyboard.
  3. Get a soundcard.
  4. Get studio monitors.
  5. get a condenser microphone.

When you get the equipments utilize their manuals to connect them effectively.

You would also need to get a D.A.W (digital audio workstation), thats were the magic happens and there are severalto choose from.

Learning Music Theory
  1. Letters of the keyboard.
  2. Chords.
  3. Scales.
  4. Chord progressions.
  5. Melodies.

Music theory is a study that allows us to understand the language of music. It is a set of guidelines and practices used to recognize the different ways to express emotions with sound.

Music theory considers melody, rhythm, counterpoint, harmony, form, tonal systems, scales, tuning, intervals, consonance, dissonance, durational proportions, the acoustics of pitch systems, composition, performance, orchestration, ornamentation, improvisation, electronic sound production.

Drum Sequencing
  1. Midi control.
  2. Steps.
  3. Patterns.
  4. Trigger notes.
  5. Programming.

Great drum sounds combined with a great drum groove can make all the difference in the way listeners interact with your music and the way different elements sit in the mix.

If you have a MIDI keyboard hooked up to your computer, your DAW probably includes some sort of software sampler that will allow you to add your samples and trigger them in real-time. You will most likely also have the option to record the MIDI information so you can further manipulate parameters. When you are programming drums you may want to adjust the velocity to give a more realistic feel or use a fixed velocity for genres where you are working with electronic drums or “drum machine” sounds.

Song Arrangement
  1. Song genre.
  2. Drums.
  3. Bass elements.
  4. Harmonic components.
  5. Song structure.

It starts off with creating an appealing and professionally sounding song arrangement – the one that will keep the listener’s attention and tell the story of the song.

You may want all the instruments to 'lock in' together and play the same rhythm; you may want them to play different rhythms. Whatever you choose to do, though, make sure this is a conscious decision, as not paying attention to rhythm will make your arrangement sound sloppy and disjointed.

Mixing and Mastering
  1. Levels balancing.
  2. Effects.
  3. Panning.
  4. Mix printing.
  5. Mastering.

Get a basic balance of your levels before you go crazy with effects processing. Trim ‘em so they don’t clip later. Think about headroom early.Keep a final goal in mind as you balance all of your tracks. This will give you a rough idea of how each track will eventually fit together. Processing will smooth out your rough ideas.

Now that you’ve learned all the ‘rules’ of mixing, break all of them. Well maybe not ALL of them. But good mixing is always creative mixing. Take chances with your mix. Mixing isn’t a textbook process you can learn overnight. It’s equal parts know-how, instincts, and risk taking. It’s one of the most creative steps in your process. Don’t squander it by following some dry, stale guide that says there’s only one way to do things. If you wanna put a flanger on your oboe, then put a flanger on your oboe!.
